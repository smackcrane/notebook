Principles of Prehab

  • ``Balance''
    • Systems that aren't used as directly in climbing are apt to be weaker than their directly used counterparts---classic examples being finger extensors vs finger flexors, or pecs/chest vs lats/back. This can lead to injuries related to weakness (possibly the case for tendonitis?), or related to imbalance (possible cause of shoulder impingement, taut internal vs floppy external rotators of the shoulder). There are some caveats here: ``but your pecs/wrist extensors/etc. are actually used in climbing!`` Nonetheless, it is clear that certain systems are trained more than others, and likely that establishing a higher baseline across the board helps to prevent injury.
  • Strength
    • This is possibly an even better way of thinking of the above note---stronger muscles and tendons are less likely to get injured, whether they are small auxiliary muscles, large antagonists, or the very protagonists of climbing. It requires some delicacy to strengthen muscles if they are heavily used in climbing as well, but can be done. My experience has been that my brachialis pain which seemed related to overuse was improved by adding a solid base of pull-ups to my training---along with other interventions such as focusing on good pulling technique, and stopping sessions earlier before reaching exhaustion.
  • Technique
    • It is an obvious and profound idea that good technique for sending is the same as good technique for injury prevention. Good technique directs as much load as possible to the strongest body parts; injury occurs when a body part is loaded more than it can accommodate. The prime example of this is foot slips -> fingers (or shoulder, knee, etc) are suddenly loaded -> injury. I've done this. I've also injured my shoulder quite directly by having poor technique, climbing without proper shoulder engagement and cutting loose (unnecessarily) putting more load than necessary trough my shoulders.

Prehab routine

  • Fingers
    • Felix and Emil's sub-max hangboarding ([1] [2] and others) is great for satisfying my desire to always be hanging on things, feeling out grip types that I'm especially weak on, and getting comfortable with a hangboard. Whether it's actually good for tendon/pulley health is quite speculative still, but seems clear that it can't hurt.
    • Rubber band finger extensions are claimed to be good for elbow health, for ``balance'' type reasons, and are easily combined with twice-a-day hangboarding.
    • Proper hard hangboarding on weak grip types. For me this is still questionable---my three finger drag is much weaker than half-crimp, and training it on the hangboard has sometimes just resulted in injury---but I'm determined to give it a proper try.
  • Wrists
    • Wrist flexor and extensor exercises are said to be good for elbow health, for ``balance'' and strength type reasons.
    • Any specific exercises inspired by this great snippet on wrist stability?
    • Spoopy thread on TFCC injuries: "blown pulleys ain't shit, broken bones no problem, but a tfcc tear is no joke" from eekabomb deep in the comments
  • Shoulders
    • Strengthen scapular retractors, for proper posture and movement patterns
    • Strengthen external rotators, for proper posture and movement patterns
    • Pull-ups, for ``strength''
    • Stretch internal rotators, for proper posture and movement patterns
    • Good posture---questionable, vs just exercising and stretching to improve posture naturally?
  • Elbows
    • Push-ups, for ``balance''
    • Pull-ups, for ``strength''
  • Hamstrings
    • A good hamstring flexibility routine---but questions as above?
    • Nordies and RDLs for strength both at the knee and at the hip. Adding different angles to get internal and external rotation will make the hams robust in a wider variety of positions.
  • Other
    • Belayer's neck exercise. Just makes my neck feel better

Schedule

  • For building strength and flexibility, 2-3 times per week (after climbing or for non-protagonists on rest days)
  • For tendon health and ``balance'' levels of strength, every day or multiple times per day
